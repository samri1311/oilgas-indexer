Bridging the Data Gap: How Transfer Learning Revolutionizes Production Forecasting in Frontier Basins - Novi Labs Skip to content Machine Learning in Oil and Gas Blog Bridging the Data Gap: How Transfer Learning Revolutionizes Production Forecasting in Frontier Basins What if you could forecast production in a frontier basin with almost no historical data? In this blog, adapted from our URTeC 2025 paper, we explore how transfer learning techniques using mature analogs like the Williston Basin can deliver reliable, calibrated forecasts in the Powder River Basin. 23 Jul 2025 Dillon Niederhut 6 min read Stay Up to Date As major North American unconventional plays approach maturity, the industry faces a critical challenge in sustaining production levels and reserve replacement. Operators increasingly turn to emerging basins both domestically and internationally, yet production forecasting in these frontier areas remains severely constrained by limited well control and production history. A groundbreaking study utilizing the Powder River Basin as a proving ground demonstrates how transfer learning methodologies can leverage rich datasets from mature basins to dramatically improve forecasting accuracy in data-sparse environments. This blog post is based on the technical paper “Can Transfer Learning Be Used to Forecast Production in Frontier Basins? A Case Study from the Powder River Basin” presented at URTeC 2025. Download the full paper here. The Emerging Basin Challenge The consolidation trend within the petroleum industry reflects the increasing scarcity of high-quality unconventional opportunities. ExxonMobil’s acquisition of Pioneer Natural Resources valued Permian acreage at approximately $60 billion, followed by Chevron’s acquisition of Hess Corporation and Occidental Petroleum’s purchase of CrownRock. These premium-priced acquisitions demonstrate the competitive intensity surrounding proven acreage positions, inherently favoring supermajors and large independents with extensive proprietary datasets and established regional operations. Independent operators pursuing alternative strategies have focused on delineating underdeveloped areas within established plays or targeting previously overlooked stratigraphic intervals. More recently, domestic operators have explored international unconventional opportunities, illustrated by Continental Resources’ joint venture to evaluate shale potential in Turkey. These ventures into less-delineated areas with limited production history present unique analytical challenges where smaller operators with higher risk tolerance may identify compelling opportunities overlooked by larger competitors. The fundamental limitation in emerging basin evaluation derives from insufficient production calibration data. Traditional petrophysical and geomechanical properties obtainable through conventional logging suites or seismic acquisition prove inadequate to fully characterize production controls. Without established production history, geological interpretations cannot be calibrated against core analysis, and even with dozens of wells, substantial uncertainty persists in delineating optimal development areas. Innovative Transfer Learning Methodology The study addresses these limitations through a methodological approach that leverages models calibrated on extensive analog basin data, then systematically modifies outputs to align with limited production history from the target basin. This technique, broadly categorized as transfer learning, encompasses multiple implementation strategies depending on specific forecasting objectives and model architectures. The research utilized the Powder River Basin as the emerging basin analog and the Williston Basin as the mature reference dataset. Comprehensive datasets were constructed comprising publicly available wellbore attributes and production histories from regulatory agencies in North Dakota, Montana, and Wyoming. These production time series were integrated with proprietary geological interpretations consisting of spatially interpolated petrophysical properties including effective porosity, water saturation, clay volume, total organic carbon content, and formation thickness. Four distinct chronological scenarios were established on January 1 of 2012, 2015, 2018, and 2021, creating experimental frameworks with progressively increasing data availability. For each scenario, Williston Basin wells constituted the analog basin training dataset, while available Powder River Basin wells formed the target basin tuning dataset. To enhance experimental validity, restrictions on available petrophysical variables were imposed based on chronological scenarios, simulating the progressive acquisition of subsurface knowledge during basin development. Advanced Calibration Approaches The methodology developed three distinct tuning approaches to calibrate Williston Basin analog forecasts to the Powder River Basin production regime. Median Shift Calibration employed a multiplicative scaling factor derived from the ratio between median analog model predictions and median observed production in the available tuning dataset. Ridge Regression Calibration utilized a regularized linear model trained to predict percentage forecast adjustments using the original feature set plus analog model predictions as additional input features. Gradient Boosting Calibration implemented a LightGBM model trained to predict forecast adjustments using combined feature sets, allowing for complex non-linear transformations. Each approach employed ensemble-based decision trees enhanced with causal-inference mechanisms designed to appropriately attribute production impacts to specific engineering and geological variables. The models incorporated methodologies to isolate treatment effects related to parent-child relationships and well spacing, conditioned on geological and completion parameters. This implementation utilized a modified Double Machine Learning approach optimized for robustness against production anomalies commonly encountered in unconventional reservoirs. Production forecast accuracy was evaluated using complementary error metrics including root mean squared error and mean absolute percentage error. This dual-metric approach provided a comprehensive assessment given the substantial heterogeneity in well productivity across the Powder River Basin through time, with different metrics offering advantages for varying production volume scenarios. Error metrics for each domain adaptation scenario Remarkable Performance Results The analysis revealed that models restricted to Powder River Basin data consistently underperformed properly calibrated analog models in generating reliable forecasts and identifying true production drivers, despite the Middle Bakken and Niobrara formations exhibiting substantially different lithological characteristics, fluid properties, and completion evolution trajectories. Across all chronological scenarios, the baseline and uncalibrated analog models consistently demonstrated the lowest and highest root mean squared error values, respectively. Performance rankings among calibration methodologies revealed nuanced patterns throughout basin development. The uncalibrated analog model provided superior forecasts with extremely limited control points, but this advantage dissipated once sufficient wells existed to establish reliable estimates of target basin median production. With expanded production history and additional petrophysical characterization, model-based calibration approaches outperformed simple scaling transformations. Consistent with statistical learning theory, lower-complexity models demonstrated advantages with limited data, while higher-complexity models performed optimally with expanded datasets. The Ridge Regression calibration outperformed all alternative approaches in the 2015 scenario with approximately 150 available control points, representing the only scenario where a model trained exclusively on Powder River Basin data underperformed in both accuracy and generalizability. The error floor for optimal forecasting approaches declined from approximately 40% in the 2012 scenario to 25% in the 2021 scenario, demonstrating progressive improvement with increased data availability and enhanced methodological sophistication. Critical Production Driver Insights The study revealed significant insights regarding feature attribution and production driver identification across development scenarios. Combined Shapley values from the Williston analog and optimal calibration models demonstrated increasing feature sensitivity through time, as expanded data availability enabled more granular delineation of production drivers. The analog-plus-calibration methodology established appropriate importance rankings for completion parameters, lateral length, and well spacing relatively early in basin development. Conversely, the baseline model lacked sufficient well design variability to learn appropriate responses until the 2018 scenario with approximately 200 control points. The baseline model consistently failed to properly characterize structural depth or other key geological parameters across all scenarios. Particularly noteworthy findings emerged regarding completion intensity responses and well spacing effects. Initially, response magnitudes appeared exaggerated as Powder River Basin completion intensities exceeded Williston Basin practices, but as the analog model better incorporated completion effects, the calibration models required less aggressive adjustments. Well spacing effects clearly demonstrated the risks of limited datasets, as forecast impacts reversed from positive to negative between 2015 and 2018 scenarios as development patterns shifted from delineation to full-field development. Changes in forecast impact in the top performing tuned model for key model features across the four scenarios Reservoir Quality Assessment Challenges The generation of integrated reservoir quality metrics through GeoSHAPS analysis revealed the complexity of separating completion design contributions from intrinsic formation properties. This differentiation remains challenging even with extensive production data, as operators typically modify completion designs based on perceived reservoir quality variations. The GeoSHAPS generated by calibration models failed to provide consistent or definitive high-grading across scenarios. Inter-scenario and inter-model differences provided valuable insights into reservoir characterization uncertainty. The Ridge Regression calibrations in 2015 and 2018 could not characterize complex relationships between completions and geological parameters, generating reservoir quality maps that essentially inverted completion intensity patterns. The 2021 scenario’s Gradient Boosting calibration favored the western basin margin, characterized by greater structural depth and lower water saturation, while the baseline model reached opposite conclusions, highlighting fundamental uncertainty in reservoir characterization with limited control points. Combined analogue and tuning model GeoSHAP’s for the joined set of training and test data PRB wells Revolutionary Industry Implications The most significant finding demonstrates that transfer learning methodologies can overcome traditional data limitations in emerging basin evaluation. The analog-plus-calibration approach delivered superior accuracy and enhanced interpretability across all development stages examined, even when analog and target formations exhibited substantially different geological characteristics. This breakthrough suggests that extensive single-basin datasets from mature plays can provide meaningful guidance for frontier basin development when properly calibrated. The optimal calibration approach followed expected patterns, with increased data availability favoring more complex models, though gradient boosting approaches performed effectively with surprisingly few control points. These findings challenge conventional industry practices that rely exclusively on target basin data for production forecasting, particularly in early development stages where data scarcity limits model reliability. The methodology presents immediate applications for operators evaluating international unconventional opportunities where well control is severely limited. By leveraging comprehensive North American datasets through appropriate transfer learning techniques, operators can generate more reliable production forecasts and identify key development drivers earlier in the evaluation process, potentially providing competitive advantages in acreage acquisition and development planning. The research establishes minimum dataset requirements for various machine learning approaches, providing practical guidelines for analog selection and model implementation. Ridge regression emerged as optimal at approximately 100 wells, while median offset calibration provided the most robust results below 10 control points, offering specific guidance for different data availability scenarios. Discover the Complete Transfer Learning Framework This analysis represents only the foundation of a comprehensive machine learning methodology that fundamentally transforms how the industry approaches production forecasting in data-limited environments. The complete 14-page technical study includes detailed domain adaptation algorithms, comprehensive Shapley value interpretation frameworks, chronological experimental design methodologies, and formation-specific calibration techniques that can be immediately implemented across diverse geological settings. The State of AI Adoption in Reservoir Engineering: 2024–2025 Comparative Analysis - Novi Labs Skip to content Blog Post The State of AI Adoption in Reservoir Engineering: 2024–2025 Comparative Analysis How has AI adoption evolved in reservoir engineering over the past year? This blog post summarizes key insights from our 2025 report, highlighting major shifts in implementation strategies, forecasting confidence, and the growing importance of proprietary data—based on responses from over 150 U. S. reservoir engineers. 24 Jun 2025 Maria Pesantez 5 min read Stay Up to Date The numbers don’t lie: reservoir engineering just crossed a line it can’t uncross. In twelve months, the industry moved from cautious AI experimentation to operational necessity. What happened between 2024 and 2025 wasn’t gradual adoption—it was transformation. Our survey of over 150 U. S. -based reservoir engineers reveals a profession that has reached a strategic inflection point. AI adoption jumped from 43% to 56%, but the real story lies beneath those numbers. This isn’t just about more companies trying AI, it’s about how they’re deploying it, where they’re concentrating resources, and what they’re learning about competitive advantage. Let’s break down what’s changed, where the momentum is building, and what it means for the future of AI in upstream oil & gas. 1. AI is No Longer Optional, It’s Operational The numbers tell a clear story: this isn’t experimentation anymore. Organizations with established analytics teams grew from 19% to 28%, while another 28% are actively building these capabilities. Combined, 56% of the industry now has dedicated AI resources, up from just 41% last year. Companies aren’t just buying software; they’re hiring data scientists and restructuring entire operations. Implementation strategies have matured alongside this commitment. Organizations have moved decisively away from risky custom-only development (declining from 35% to 22%) toward proven hybrid approaches that blend commercial solutions with targeted customization (increasing from 38% to 53%). These hybrid approaches consistently deliver the highest success rates at 72%, proving that balanced strategies outperform extremes. Beyond infrastructure investment, the real transformation lies in how companies deploy AI: Well forecasting dominates the landscape at 75%, up from 58%, a 17-point jump that shows companies doubling down where AI delivers the biggest impact on capital allocation decisions. Data analysis and visualization surged to 64%, up 13 points, reflecting the growing importance of robust data workflows in AI-driven operations. Development planning climbed 8 points to 32%, indicating AI is moving beyond forecasting into strategic decision-making across the value chain. The takeaway? The transformation runs deeper than adoption rates suggest. Engineers have moved beyond proof-of-concept thinking. AI isn’t something they’re testing, it’s becoming embedded in the core workflows that drive real business decisions. The experimental phase is over. The operational phase has begun. 2. The Data Dilemma is Getting Louder The data quality crisis is deepening, not improving, despite rapid AI advances. Engineers are reporting more upstream public data issues now than they did before the AI-driven analytics adoption wave. This reveals a fundamental constraint: sophisticated algorithms amplify garbage data rather than clean it up. Poor data quality remains the #1 barrier to successful AI adoption The numbers paint a stark picture. 84% of respondents now report challenges with upstream public data quality and accessibility, up from 76% last year. But here’s what’s really changing: who owns the problem. 74% of reservoir engineers now say they’re primarily responsible for data quality, up from 65%. The industry is shifting data governance away from IT departments and toward the people who actually suffer when the data is wrong. It’s a logical evolution. When your forecasts fail because of messy datasets, you stop trusting someone else to fix it. Engineers are taking control because they have to live with the consequences. The results validate this shift, organizations where engineers lead data quality report significantly higher AI success rates than those where IT retains control. 3. Trust in Traditional Methods Is Slipping Confidence in type curves and traditional forecasting methods is eroding fast. In 2024, 39% of engineers felt highly confident in traditional forecasting tools. In 2025, only 23% still do. This decline isn’t happening in a vacuum. Engineers are increasingly recognizing inherent limitations, with 74% now believing type curves are prone to bias and slowness, up 13 points from last year. When engineers compare traditional forecasting with ML-driven approaches head-to-head, the results are decisive: 60% say AI-driven forecasts perform better, while only 11% favor traditional methods. Yet a significant implementation gap persists. 37% of respondents haven’t tried machine learning-based forecasting at all. The disconnect is striking: while most engineers intellectually accept AI’s superiority, more than a third still haven’t taken the step to test it themselves. Experience is replacing skepticism, but only for those willing to experiment. 4. Proprietary Data Is Becoming the New Battleground The most consequential shift may be this: competitive advantage has migrated from algorithms to data assets. Organizations using proprietary data report 73% success rates with AI forecasting, compared to just 41% for those relying exclusively on public sources. This 32-percentage-point differential represents a sustainable competitive advantage that cannot be easily replicated. This shift is reinforced by rising frustration with public data: Quality issues surged from 76% to 84%, an 8-point increase that undermines the foundation of any AI initiative Accessibility problems jumped 13 points from 71% to 84%, making it harder to even obtain the data needed for analysis, forcing engineers to cobble together information from multiple fragmented sources Completeness concerns rose 12 points from 73% to 85%, meaning engineers are working with increasingly incomplete datasets Consistency challenges climbed 10 points from 68% to 78%, creating additional complexity in data preparation and validation When every aspect of public data is deteriorating simultaneously, proprietary data becomes not just advantageous, it becomes essential for competitive survival. The implication is clear: organizations that can build proprietary data ecosystems will establish lasting advantages in forecasting accuracy and capital planning effectiveness. Those relying solely on public sources will find themselves systematically disadvantaged as data quality continues to decline. 5. Engineers Are Reskilling Faster Than Their Organizations The reservoir engineering profession is transforming at remarkable speed. Engineers with data science expertise jumped from 54% to 74% in one year, a 20-percentage-point surge that indicates rapid professional adaptation. Those with no interest in these capabilities nearly vanished, dropping from 11% to 1%. . But while engineers are reskilling, company support hasn’t kept up: 45% of companies still offer no formal training in AI, data science, or analytics. Only 8% provide comprehensive development programs. The mismatch signals a looming challenge: organizations that invest in systematic upskilling will win not just in AI deployment, but in talent retention and innovation velocity. Engineers are reskilling themselves, whether companies help or not. Organizations that support this transition will keep their best people. Those who ignore it will lose them to competitors. Where Are We Headed? The profession has reached a tipping point in its view of AI’s role. 60% of engineers now believe AI will become standard practice in reservoir engineering within the next decade, a decisive shift from 45% in 2024. This 15-percentage-point jump represents more than changing opinion; it signals emerging consensus that AI adoption is inevitable rather than optional. The future is defined by: Hybrid AI strategies that balance flexibility and scalability Proprietary data ecosystems that unlock competitive insights Cross-functional collaboration between engineers and data scientists Reskilling at scale to match rising expectations and complexity Organizations that can align people, tools, and data will lead the next phase of oil & gas innovation. The Powder River Basin covers approximately 43, 000 square miles, covering parts of northeastern Wyoming and southeastern Montana. Major counties within the basin include Campbell, Converse, Johnson, and Sheridan in Wyoming, and Powder River and Rosebud in Montana. The Powder River itself is a tributary of the Yellowstone River, originating in the Bighorn Mountains in Wyoming. The river flows northward into Montana and is approximately 375 miles long. The river and its basin have given their name to the Powder River Basin. Oil Production Forecasting Methods Oil production forecasting can be approached through several methodologies, each with its own strengths and limitations. Traditional methods like decline curve analysis (DCA) remain widely used for their simplicity and transparency, while physics-based approaches such as material balance and reservoir simulation provide deeper technical rigor. More recently, machine learning and hybrid methods have emerged, offering scalable, data-driven forecasts that capture complex patterns in unconventional plays. Below, we break down the key forecasting methods used across the industry. Traditional Decline Curve Analysis (DCA) Decline Curve Analysis (DCA) is the most widely used forecasting method in oil and gas. It models historical production trends using mathematical equations to estimate future output. The most common form is the Arps equation, developed in the 1940s, which includes three types of decline curves: When It Works Best When It Falls Short DCA is highly effective when: The well has sufficient production history (typically 12+ months) Flow regimes are stable (no major shut-ins or changes in operation) There’s minimal interference from parent/child wells or offset activity It’s commonly used in PDP valuation, reserve classification, and post-drill performance analysis. DCA becomes less reliable when: Forecasting early-life wells with <6 months of history Modeling wells in high-density shale plays with spacing interference Predicting long-term behavior in parent-child environments Data quality is inconsistent (e. g. allocated or noisy data) In these cases, curve-fitting alone may oversimplify production behavior, leading to over- or underestimated EURs. Static vs. Dynamic Decline Curves Most traditional workflows rely on static DCA, a one-time curve fit based on current data. However, modern tools are moving toward dynamic DCA, where decline parameters are updated as new production data becomes available. This allows forecasts to remain current without manual rework, making them more reliable in fast-changing field conditions. Material Balance & Simulation Material balance analysis and reservoir simulation are classical physics-based methods used primarily for conventional fields or large, well-characterized reservoirs. Reservoir simulation models are essential tools for analyzing complex reservoir conditions, managing production, and optimizing recovery strategies. While less common in shale development, they remain valuable tools in full-field modeling, secondary recovery planning, and reserves reporting. Material Balance: Reservoir Volume and Pressure Matching Material balance analysis relies on the principle of mass conservation to estimate original hydrocarbons in place (OHIP) and predict future performance. By tracking production and reservoir pressure over time, engineers can estimate reservoir drive mechanisms (e. g. , depletion, water drive) and forecast long-term production. This method is highly effective in: Conventional reservoirs with good pressure data Waterfloods or gas drives Fields with few wells and stable production trends However, it’s not practical in shale basins where pressure data is sparse, and transient flow dominates early-time performance. Reservoir Simulation: Static and Dynamic Models Reservoir simulation uses 3D models of the subsurface, built from geological, petrophysical, and engineering data, to simulate fluid flow across a grid. These models are either: Static – representing the reservoir structure and properties Dynamic – incorporating well activity, production, pressure, and time-dependent behaviors Simulation is the most detailed and physics-rich forecasting approach. It’s essential for: Complex reservoirs with heterogeneity or multiple phases History matching and development planning Secondary and tertiary recovery modeling But this depth comes with trade-offs: simulations are time-consuming, data-intensive, and hard to scale across hundreds of wells or pads, making them impractical for large shale portfolios without significant simplification. Machine Learning Forecasting Machine learning (ML) forecasting has emerged as one of the most powerful and scalable approaches for predicting oil production, especially in unconventional plays like the Permian, Eagle Ford, and STACK. Unlike traditional methods that rely solely on curve-fitting or physics-based modeling, ML uses large datasets to learn complex relationships between inputs (like completions, geology, and spacing) and production outcomes. Common machine learning models for oil production forecasting include support vector machines (SVMs), support vector regression (SVR), and artificial neural networks (ANNs) modeling, which are widely used for their ability to handle nonlinear relationships and improve prediction accuracy. What Makes ML Forecasting Different? Machine learning models are data-driven. They don’t assume a pre-set decline shape like Arps or require full reservoir characterization like simulation. Instead, they use historical production and engineering data to detect patterns and forecast future performance, often outperforming traditional models in early-life wells, child-parent interactions, or complex development areas. ML forecasting excels when: You need to forecast thousands of wells at once You’re working with short production histories You want to include spacing, proppant, geology, or vintage as input features You’re modeling development scenarios across a basin or portfolio How It Works in Practice In platforms like Novi Labs, engineers train models using both public and proprietary data across multiple operators and basins. Input variables might include: Lateral length Proppant per foot Stage spacing Landing zone TOC, porosity, pressure Completion vintage Distance to nearest offset (for interference) Once trained, the model can forecast performance for new wells, even those with no production history, by applying learnings from analog wells with similar characteristics. Parent-Child Interactions and Spacing Awareness One of ML’s biggest advantages is its ability to model spacing effects and well interference. Traditional DCA assumes each well is independent, but ML models can learn how production degrades in child wells or when wells are drilled too close together. This allows teams to forecast with much greater accuracy in dense, cube-style developments. Dynamic, Adaptive, and Transferable ML models are adaptive, they get smarter as more data becomes available. And with techniques like transfer learning, models trained in one basin (e. g. , Midland Wolfcamp A) can help improve forecasts in geologically similar areas with sparse data. Modern forecasting teams use ML not to replace engineering judgment, but to enhance it, providing faster, deeper insights that scale across assets, teams, and development strategies. Hybrid Forecasting Methods Hybrid forecasting combines the strengths of both physics-based and machine learning methods to produce more accurate and explainable oil production forecasts, especially in complex or data-limited environments. Rather than relying on a single modeling philosophy, hybrid approaches integrate engineering principles (like decline trends, reservoir mechanics, and spacing effects) with data-driven models trained on thousands of analog wells. Particle swarm optimization is often used to optimize parameters in hybrid models for oil production forecasting, enhancing global search capabilities, convergence speed, and parameter tuning efficiency in complex models such as TCN-KAN. This allows for the generation of forecasts that are both statistically robust and technically grounded. Why Combine ML with Physics? Each method has its own strengths: Physics-based models (e. g. Arps, simulation) are transparent and defensible, but often struggle in early-life wells or non-traditional flow regimes. Machine learning models excel at capturing complex patterns and interactions, but can be perceived as black boxes. Hybrid forecasting allows engineers to apply engineering assumptions (like terminal decline rates, flattening curves, or economic limits) while still taking advantage of machine learning’s accuracy and speed. Common Hybrid Approaches Include: ML for early-life forecasting, transitioning to DCA for long-term tail behavior Decline curve fitting on top of ML predictions, ensuring familiarity in shape and confidence for reserves reporting Physics-informed feature engineering, where reservoir parameters guide model inputs (e. g. using b-factor estimates or pressure gradients) Post-processing ML outputs with engineer-adjustable limits or constraints Balancing Automation with Engineering Control A key benefit of hybrid forecasting is that it provides: Speed and scale across thousands of wells Transparency and interpretability for technical reviews Customizability for scenario planning, development design, or economic modeling In tools like Novi, this hybrid approach allows engineers to edit forecast shapes, apply their own logic, and trace back every input, ensuring forecasts are not just accurate, but also auditable and defensible. What Are P10, P50, and P90 Forecasts? Probabilistic forecasts generate a distribution of outcomes instead of a single curve. The most common outputs are: P10 – a high-side case (there’s a 10% chance production exceeds this value) P50 – the most likely case (the median forecast) P90 – a conservative case (there’s a 90% chance production exceeds this value) These scenarios are especially valuable in: Reserve classification and booking A&D screening and risk evaluation Economic modeling and debt financing How Are Probabilistic Curves Generated? There are several ways to build probabilistic forecasts: Statistical modeling of production from analog wells Monte Carlo simulations that vary key parameters Machine learning models that learn the shape and spread of uncertainty from historical data Clustering and binning techniques that generate distributions across landing zones, vintages, or operator groups In platforms like Novi Labs, users can generate P10/P50/P90 type curves instantly by selecting a group of analog wells. The system automatically calculates the distribution of outcomes, removing the need for manual band-fitting or spreadsheet-based curve blending. Why It Matters for Real-World Decisions Probabilistic forecasting provides: A more realistic view of uncertainty and risk Confidence bands for investor presentations or reserves audits Inputs for scenario-based economic modeling A better understanding of variability across benches, zones, and development styles By embracing probabilistic thinking, operators and investors can avoid overconfidence in single-point forecasts, and instead make more resilient, data-backed decisions. Get access to Alberta's Data Overview Alberta is the powerhouse of Canada’s oil and gas industry, contributing the majority of the country's fossil fuel production. Alberta’s Athabasca, Peace River, and Cold Lake oil sands contain an estimated 165 billion barrels of recoverable oil, making them the third-largest oil reserves in the world. These oil and gas resources are primarily located within the Western Canadian Sedimentary Basin, which is the main source of Canada’s oil and natural gas. Alberta produces over 80% of Canada’s crude oil and 65% of Canada's natural gas Alberta has historically been one of the largest suppliers of natural gas to the U. S. , with major pipeline systems like the Nova Gas Transmission System (NGTL) and TransCanada pipelines. Ontario is also a major destination for Alberta’s oil and gas products, serving as a key point in the distribution network. Where is Alberta? Alberta is a province in western Canada, located between British Columbia to the west and Saskatchewan to the east, with the U. S. state of Montana forming its southern border. To the north, it extends toward Canada’s Northwest Territories. Alberta is part of the Prairie Provinces, though its landscape varies from vast plains to the towering Rocky Mountains in the west. The province’s capital is Edmonton, while Calgary, its largest city, is the economic center for the oil and gas industry. Alberta: History, Data and Facts Oil production in Alberta began around the year 1914. Alberta's Oil Production When and how did it start? Crude oil production in Alberta began in the early 1900s, with billions of barrels produced since, making the province a cornerstone of Canada’s energy supply. The first significant discovery came in 1914 at Turner Valley, south of Calgary. While important, technological limitations prevented large-scale development. Alberta’s oil industry truly accelerated in 1947, when the Leduc No. 1 well near Edmonton triggered a major oil boom. This discovery established Alberta as Canada’s leading oil producer, supported by new fields and the rapid expansion of infrastructure such as pipelines, straddle plants, and processing facilities. By the 1960s, attention shifted to Alberta’s oil sands, one of the largest petroleum reserves in the world. Initial projects focused on surface mining, later evolving into in-situ extraction techniques that could recover deeper, heavier crude. Oil sands development required advanced methods to process the viscous resource, but these innovations created long-term growth opportunities for the province. Supporting facilities and refineries became critical for transforming crude oil and natural gas into usable products. These plants produce transportation fuels such as gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, while also supplying asphalt, heating products, and electricity. Today, crude oil and natural gas remain central to Alberta’s energy mix and economic output. Alberta’s oil and gas industry continues to expand its reach to global markets through new infrastructure, including liquefied natural gas (LNG) terminals and export facilities. Provincial agencies such as Alberta Energy and the Alberta Energy Regulator oversee mineral rights, environmental standards, and emissions management to ensure responsible and sustainable development. Over more than a century, Alberta has evolved from early discoveries at Turner Valley to becoming a global leader in oil production, oil sands innovation, and energy exports.
